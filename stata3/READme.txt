part 1
A fixed population of 10,000 individuals was created. The independent variable X and z, and Y is the dependent variable. The complete formula is Y = 1.5X + Z. A simulation was performed by sampling from this population with replacement at four different sample sizes (N = 10, 100, 1000, 10000). For each sample size, we estimated the regression of Y on X 500 times, storing the b coefficient, the standard error of the mean, the p-value, and the 95% confidence intervals, resulting in a final dataset of 2,000 regressions.
At $N=10, the estimates for b exhibit extreme variance, frequently diverging far from the true parameter. As the sample size increases, the estimates converge tightly. The boxplot clearly visualizes this convergence; the interquartile range of the estimates shrinks dramatically as $N$ grows, demonstrating the property of consistency.
The standard error decreases as the N increases, and it decreases at a rate proportional to 1/square root of N.
Because the confidence intervals are directly tied to the SEM, the width of the confidence intervals narrows as N gets larger. At N=10, the intervals are exceptionally wide, leading to higher uncertainty about the true parameter. At N=10000, the upper and lower bounds are practically identical to the estimate itself.

part 2
As shown in "beta_variation",  at drastically small sample sizes (N < 30), the estimates for b exhibit heavy variance. The point estimates generated from these small samples often drift significantly away from the true population parameter of 2, due strictly to random sampling noise. However, as N increases, the standard deviation of our calculated betas rapidly shrinks. By the time the sample size reaches the thousands and beyond, the b estimates converge tightly onto the true parameter.
As N gets larger, the Standard Error of the Mean decreases.  At N=4 and N=10, the 95% confidence intervals are extremely wide, reflecting high uncertainty about the true population parameter. However, as the sample size scales up to 10,000 and eventually 1,000,000, the confidence intervals collapse inward until they are practically indistinguishable from the true estimate. With massive sample sizes, sampling noise effectively disappears, resulting in standard errors that approach 0.000 and p-values that decisively reject the null hypothesis.
For part 1, because I created a static population dataset with a fixed N, that is, my statistical universe is permanently capped at those 10,000 unique individuals. Any sample larger than 10,000 would simply be a heavy recycling of the same finite data points, which does not add new information to the system. For part 2, I removed the fixed n, and I used the program "set obs n" and randomly generated fresh values from the normal distribution directly in Stata's memory for every single simulation.
Because Part 2 has a much higher error variance and a much lower variance in the independent variable X, the SEM in Part 2 will be roughly 10 times larger than in Part 1 for any given sample size. Consequently, the confidence intervals in Part 2 will be significantly wider.
