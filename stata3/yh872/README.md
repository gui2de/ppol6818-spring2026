### Part 1 - Sampling Noise in a Fixed Population

**Overview** In Part 1, I established a Data Generating Process (DGP) to create a fixed population of 10,000 individual observations. The true underlying relationship was defined as $Y = 5 + 2X + \epsilon$, meaning the true population parameter for $\beta$ is 2. I then simulated a sampling and regression process 500 times across four distinct sample sizes ($N=10$, $N=100$, $N=1000$, and $N=10,000$) to observe the behavior of sampling noise.

**Results & Analysis** \* **Variation in Beta Estimates (Figure 1):** The box plot visually demonstrates how sampling noise diminishes as sample size increases. At $N=10$, there is substantial sampling variability, resulting in a wide spread of estimated beta coefficients (ranging from a minimum of 1.2486 to a maximum of 2.5691). As $N$ increases to 100 and 1,000, the estimates tightly converge around the true $\beta$ of 2. \* **SEM and Confidence Intervals (Table 1):** The summary statistics table quantifies this convergence. As the sample size increases, the mean Standard Error of the Mean (SEM) drops significantly, falling from 0.1817 at $N=10$ down to 0.0160 at $N=1000$. Consequently, the average 95% confidence interval width narrows drastically (from 0.8379 to 0.0627), reflecting a much higher degree of precision and certainty in our estimates with larger samples. \* **The Full Population Census (**$N=10,000$): At $N=10,000$, the empirical variance of our beta estimates (`sd_beta`) drops to exactly 0.0000. Because our sample size equals the entire fixed population size, and I are sampling without replacement, every one of the 500 iterations essentially conducts a full census of the exact same 10,000 observations. Therefore, the regression yields the exact same coefficient ($\beta = 2.0010$) every single time, eliminating sampling noise entirely. While the regression still reports a mathematical SEM (0.0051) and confidence interval width (0.0198) based on the OLS formula, the actual variation across the samples is zero.

### Part 2 - Sampling Noise in an Infinite Superpopulation

**Overview** In Part 2, I modified our simulation so that the Data Generating Process (DGP) occurs *inside* the program itself. Instead of sampling from a pre-generated, static dataset, each iteration of the simulation dynamically generates a completely new set of observations directly from the underlying mathematical distributions ($X \sim \mathcal{N}(50, 10)$ and $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 5)$). This simulates sampling from an infinite superpopulation.

**Results & Analysis (Infinite Population)** \* **Variation in Beta Estimates:** As visualized in the Part 2 box plot, the spread of our estimated beta coefficients steadily shrinks as the sample size grows exponentially. At very small sample sizes (e.g., $N=4$ and $N=8$), the estimates are highly volatile. However, as $N$ scales up into the millions, the distribution flattens into a perfect line exactly on the true beta of 2. \* **SEM and Confidence Intervals:** The summary table confirms this visual trend. At $N=4$, the mean Standard Error of the Mean (SEM) is large at 0.3495, yielding a massive average confidence interval width of 3.0073. As $N$ increases to $2,097,152$, the mean SEM approaches zero (0.0003), and the CI width shrinks to just 0.0014, demonstrating the Law of Large Numbers.

### Part 3 - Power Calculations for Individual-Level Randomization

**Overview** In Part 3, I explored power calculations for an individual-level Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT). I developed a Data Generating Process (DGP) where the baseline outcome $Y$ is normally distributed around 0 with a standard deviation of 1. To model a realistic, heterogeneous intervention, the treatment effect was uniformly distributed between 0.0 and 0.2 SD, resulting in an Average Treatment Effect (ATE) of exactly 0.1 SD.

**1. Base Sample Size Calculation (50% Treatment Allocation)** Our baseline goal was to calculate the number of individuals required to detect a 0.1 SD treatment effect with 80% statistical power, assuming an equal 50/50 split between the treatment and control groups. \* Using standard power calculations for a two-sample means test (`power twomeans`), the required total sample size is **3,142** individuals (1,571 in the control arm and 1,571 in the treatment arm). \* *Simulation Verification:* I verified this mathematically by simulating the DGP 1,000 times with $N=3,142$. Our empirical power (the proportion of simulations where the regression p-value was less than 0.05) closely matched the 80% target.

**2. Adjusting for 15% Attrition** In real-world RCTs, participants often drop out before the endline survey. If I anticipate a 15% attrition rate equally across both arms, our starting sample must be larger so that our *final effective* sample remains 3,142. \* To calculate this, I divide our target sample size by the expected retention rate ($1 - 0.15 = 0.85$). \* Calculation: $3,142 / 0.85 = 3,696.47$. \* Therefore, I must recruit and randomize **3,697** individuals at baseline to maintain 80% power after 15% of the sample attrites.

**3. Adjusting for 30% Treatment Allocation (Expensive Intervention)** If the intervention is too expensive to provide to half the sample, and I can only afford to treat 30% of participants, the statistical efficiency of our experiment decreases. An unbalanced design requires a larger overall sample size to achieve the same power. \* The allocation ratio of the treatment group ($N_2$) to the control group ($N_1$) is 30/70, which is approximately $0.4286$. \* Using the `nratio(0.42857)` option in Stata's `power` command, I held power constant at 80% and the detectable effect size at 0.1 SD. \* The new required sample size jumps to **3,740** total individuals. \* This breaks down to **2,618** individuals in the control group and **1,122** individuals receiving the treatment.

### Part 4 - Power Calculations for Cluster Randomization

**Overview** In Part 4, I transitioned from individual-level randomization to a cluster-randomized design where treatment assignment occurs at the school level. To simulate this accurately, I generated a data structure with an Intraclass Correlation (ICC) of approximately 0.3, meaning 30% of the variance in math scores is explained by school-level differences, while 70% is explained by individual student differences.

**Q5: Holding Clusters Fixed (200 Schools), Increasing Cluster Size** To observe the effect of cluster size on statistical power, I fixed the total number of schools at 200 (100 treatment, 100 control) with a target treatment effect of 0.2 SD. I then scaled the number of students per school exponentially from 2 up to 1024. \* **The Plateau Effect:** The empirical power starts very low at 0.4280 for a cluster size of 2. It rises sharply at first, reaching 0.6900 at a cluster size of 16. However, the graph clearly demonstrates a plateau effect where the curve flattens out. Even when the cluster size is massively increased to 1024 students per school, the power only reaches 0.7460. The empirical power curve remains entirely below the 80% target threshold. \* **Recommendation:** Because the ICC is quite high (0.3), students within the same school are highly correlated. Surveying more students in the same school provides severely diminishing marginal returns in terms of statistical information. Based on these results, I would recommend a cluster size of roughly **16 to 32 students per school**. Beyond this point, the gains in power are negligible relative to the likely cost of surveying hundreds of extra students. To successfully reach 80% power in this scenario, I cannot simply survey more students per school; I must increase the total number of clusters (schools) in the experiment.

**Q6: Schools Needed for 80% Power (Fixed Cluster Size = 15)** To determine the required number of schools when the cluster size is fixed at 15 students per school, I used analytical power calculations adjusting for an Intraclass Correlation (ICC) of 0.3. The clustering creates a large Design Effect ($DEFF = 1 + (15-1) \times 0.3 = 5.2$), meaning I need approximately 5.2 times more total students than a standard individual-level RCT. \* To achieve 80% power to detect a 0.2 SD treatment effect, I need exactly **274 total schools** (137 in the control group and 137 in the treatment group). \* This results in a total sample size of 4,110 students.

**Q7: Adjusting for 70% Adoption (Imperfect Compliance)** If only 70% of the assigned schools actually adopt the treatment, our effective Intention-to-Treat (ITT) effect shrinks from 0.20 SD to 0.14 SD. Because sample size requirements scale inversely with the square of the effect size, this seemingly small drop in compliance drastically reduces our statistical efficiency. \* Holding the cluster size at 15 and the ICC at 0.3, the required number of schools roughly doubles to compensate for the lost effect size. \* I now need **556 total schools** (278 control and 278 treatment) to maintain 80% power, bringing the total required student sample size to 8,340.
